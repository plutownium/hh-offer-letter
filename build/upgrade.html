Four Free Ways to Improve Your Resume Today

If the free advice is good, what's the full service like?

Tip #1: Use a two or three page resume.

The “It has to fit on one page” advice is bullshit.

Of the fifty candidates I helped get hired this year, 49 of them used a two or three page resume.

The truth is that if you use a one page resume, employers will presume that’s all you had to offer.

Are you using a one page resume right now?

So long as you follow the next tips, you’ll see an improvement by adding a second page. (Supposing you don’t adhere to them already, anyway.)

Tip #2: Use a simple low-design approach.

While you don’t want to present a document with zero formatting, making the resume flashy is also the wrong approach.

Recruiters will throw out a resume that looks flashy before they even read it.

You want your features and benefits to stand on their own.

The design takes attention away from the bullet points.

So by using low-design, you allow the bullet points to “speak for themselves.”

Tip #3: Use language from the job post you are applying to.

It takes far more time, but the upside is huge: Recruiters will recognize

Your application really was crafted specifically for that posting.

Your skills really match exactly what they asked for.

Without your application matching the job post’s language…

The recruitment team has to do a lot of thinking to understand how you’d fill the role.

When you use language that matches the job post, they understand exactly:

“Oh yeah, s/he would be able to fulfill requirements X, Y and Z.”

That means you’ll get more interviews.

That means you’ll be in demand.

That means you’ll be offered more money.

Tip #4: Using specific detailed bullet points that are quantified.

If you aren’t in the habit of doing this, it will take work to start doing it.

But…

It will be well worth the effort.

Don’t say: “Reduced call time from the database.”

Do say: “Reduced call time from the database by 37% by decreasing read algorithms from O(nlogn) to O(n).”

Don’t say: “Decreased page load speed by converting to Tailwind, yielding a decrease in the bounce rate.”

Do say: “Decreased page load speed by 48% by converting from CSS to Tailwind, yielding a 26% decrease in the bounce rate.”

Similar to Tip #2, the issue is that the vague option leaves recruiters to fill in the blanks themselves.

(And they don’t fill them in. Because it would take effort and require presumptions.)

By telling them specifically, quantifiably, what you changed during your previous roles, you go from…

“Meh, not sure what this means.”

To…

“Wow, that would be such an improvement here.”

These tips work and work well.

Applying them will get you a better response rate immediately.
